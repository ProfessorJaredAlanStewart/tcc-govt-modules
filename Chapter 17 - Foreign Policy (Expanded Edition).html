üéØ Section 1: Defining Foreign Policy

Understanding the goals, types, and challenges of U.S. foreign policy

üåç What is Foreign Policy?

When we consider policy as our chapter focus, we are looking broadly at the actions the U.S. government carries out for particular purposes. In the case of foreign policy, that purpose is to manage its relationships with other nations of the world. Policy results from a course of action or a pattern of actions over time, rather than from a single action or decision.

For example, U.S. foreign policy with Russia has been forged by several presidents, as well as by cabinet secretaries, House and Senate members, and foreign policy agency bureaucrats. Policy is also purposive, or intended to do something‚Äîthat is, policymaking is not random. When the United States enters into an international agreement with other countries on aims such as free trade or nuclear disarmament, it does so for specific reasons.

üèõÔ∏è Foreign Policy
The goals that a state's officials seek to attain abroad, the values that give rise to those objectives, and the means or instruments used to pursue them. Foreign policy is externally focused, while domestic policy sets strategies internal to the United States.
üîç Click to explore: Foreign vs. Domestic Policy

+25 points! Great exploration!

We distinguish foreign policy, which is externally focused, from domestic policy, which sets strategies internal to the United States. However, the two types of policies can become quite intertwined.

For example, one might talk about Latino politics as a domestic issue when considering educational policies designed to increase the number of Hispanic Americans who attend and graduate from a U.S. college or university. However, Latino politics can quickly become a foreign policy matter when considering topics such as immigration from and foreign trade with countries in Central America and South America.

üéØ The Four Main Objectives of U.S. Foreign Policy

While the goals of a nation's foreign policy are always open to debate and revision, there are nonetheless four main goals to which we can attribute much of what the U.S. government does in the foreign policy realm:

The Four Goals:

  1. Protection of the U.S. and its citizens - Including protection of allies and responding to military and economic threats
  2. Maintenance of access to key resources and markets - Ensuring access to natural resources, foreign investment, and international trade
  3. Preservation of a balance of power in the world - Promoting stability and preventing any one nation or region from becoming too powerful
  4. Protection of human rights and democracy - Supporting international peace and participating in organizations like the United Nations
üîç Click to learn more: Goal 1 - Protection

+25 points! Understanding U.S. security!

The first goal is the protection of the United States and the lives of its citizens, both while they are in the United States and when they travel abroad. Related to this security goal is the aim of protecting the country's allies, or countries with which the United States is friendly and mutually supportive.

In the international sphere, threats and dangers can take several forms, including military threats from other nations or terrorist groups and economic threats from boycotts and high tariffs on trade.

An economic sanction occurs when a country or multiple countries suspend trade or other financial relationships with another country to signal their displeasure with the behavior of that country.

üîç Click to learn more: Goal 2 - Resources & Markets

+25 points! Understanding economic interests!

The second main goal of U.S. foreign policy is to ensure the nation maintains access to key resources and markets across the world. Resources include natural resources, such as oil, and economic resources, including the infusion of foreign capital investment for U.S. domestic infrastructure projects.

Of course, access to the international marketplace also means access to goods that American consumers might want, such as Swiss chocolate and Australian wine. U.S. foreign policy also seeks to advance the interests of U.S. business, to both sell domestic products in the international marketplace and support general economic development around the globe.

üîç Click to learn more: Goal 3 - Balance of Power

+25 points! Understanding global stability!

A third main goal is the preservation of a balance of power in the world. A balance of power means no one nation or region is much more powerful militarily than are the countries of the rest of the world.

During the Cold War era that followed World War II, stability was achieved by the existence of dual superpowers, the United States and the Soviet Union, and by the real fear of the nuclear annihilation of which both were capable.

Today, in the post‚ÄìCold War era, many parts of Europe are politically more free, but carefully planned acts of terrorism have introduced a new type of enemy‚Äînonstate or nongovernmental organizations, such as al-Qaeda and ISIS, consisting of various terrorist cells located in many different countries.

üîç Click to learn more: Goal 4 - Human Rights & Democracy

+25 points! Understanding humanitarian goals!

The fourth main goal of U.S. foreign policy is the protection of human rights and democracy. The payoff of stability that comes from other U.S. foreign policy goals is peace and tranquility.

While certainly looking out for its own strategic interests, the United States nonetheless attempts to support international peace through many aspects of its foreign policy, such as foreign aid, and through its support of and participation in international organizations such as the United Nations, NATO (the North Atlantic Treaty Organization), and the Organization of American States.

üåê The United Nations

The United Nations (UN) is perhaps the foremost international organization in the world today. The main institutional bodies of the UN are the General Assembly and the Security Council.

üèõÔ∏è UN Structure
  • General Assembly: Includes all member nations and admits new members and approves the UN budget by a two-thirds majority
  • Security Council: Includes fifteen countries, five permanent members (including the United States) and ten non-permanent rotating members. Makes all decisions related to international peace and security.
  • International Court of Justice: Located in The Hague (Netherlands)
  • UN Secretariat: Includes the Secretary-General and UN staff

üìú Milestone: The Creation of the United Nations

After the failed attempt to formalize a "League of Nations" in the wake of World War I, and on the heels of a worldwide depression that began in 1929, came World War II, history's deadliest military conflict. The scope of the conflict and Hitler's nearly successful attempt to take over Europe entirely struck fear in minds and hearts.

The war brought about a sea change in international relations and governance, from the Marshall Plan to rebuild Europe, to NATO that created a cross-national military shield for Western Europe, to the creation of the UN in 1945, when the representatives of fifty countries met and signed the Charter of the United Nations in San Francisco, California.

Today, the United Nations, headquartered in New York City, includes 193 of the 195 nations of the world.

üìã Types of Foreign Policy

The United States pursues its four main foreign policy goals through several different foreign policy types, or distinct substantive areas of foreign policy:

Policy Type Description
Trade Policy How the U.S. interacts with other countries to ease the flow of commerce and goods. Ranges from protectionism to free trade.
Diplomacy The establishment and maintenance of formal relationships between countries through ambassadors and embassies.
Sanctions Economic penalties imposed on countries to signal displeasure with their behavior. Includes trade restrictions and asset freezes.
Military/Defense Using military power to achieve foreign policy objectives, from deterrence to active engagement.
Intelligence Overt and covert gathering of information from foreign sources that might be of strategic interest.
Foreign Aid Providing material and economic assistance to other countries, especially developing nations.
Global Environmental Policy Addressing world-level environmental matters such as climate change, ozone depletion, and ocean pollution.
üí° Hard Power vs. Soft Power

Soft power refers to nonmilitary tools to influence another country, like economic sanctions and diplomacy.

Hard power refers to the use of military power.

‚ö†Ô∏è Unique Challenges in Foreign Policy

U.S. foreign policy is a massive and complex enterprise. Here are its unique challenges:

üîç Challenge 1: No World-Level Authority

+25 points! Understanding the global challenge!

There exists no true world-level authority dictating how the nations of the world should relate to one another. If one nation negotiates in bad faith or lies to another, there is no central government authority to sanction that country.

The European Union is the single supranational entity with some real and significant authority over its member nations, but even it has enforcement issues, as evidenced by struggles within its ranks.

üîç Challenge 2: Differing Views on Government's Role

+25 points! Understanding diverse governance!

A second challenge is the widely differing views among countries about the role of government in people's lives. The government of hardline communist North Korea regulates everything in its people's lives every day. At the other end of the spectrum are countries with little government activity at all.

This domestic relationship between a government and its people can often become intertwined with foreign policy.

üîç Challenge 3: Varying Forms of Government

+25 points! Understanding government diversity!

A third unique challenge is other countries' varying ideas about the appropriate form of government. These forms range from democracies on one side to various authoritarian forms on the other.

Relations between the United States and democratic states tend to operate more smoothly, proceeding from the shared core assumption that government's authority comes from the people. Monarchies and nondemocratic forms of government do not share this assumption.

üîç Challenge 4: Transnational Issues

+25 points! Understanding modern challenges!

A fourth challenge is that many new foreign policy issues transcend borders. There are no longer simply friendly states and enemy states. Problems around the world that might affect the United States, such as terrorism, the international slave trade, and climate change, originate with groups and issues that are not country-specific. They are transnational.

The U.S. war against terrorism has been aimed at terrorist groups that do not fit neatly within the borders of any one country with which the United States could quickly interact to solve the problem.

üîç Challenge 5: Varying Country Conditions

+25 points! Understanding development disparities!

The fifth and final unique challenge is the varying conditions of the countries in the world and their effect on what is possible in terms of foreign policy and diplomatic relations.

Relations between the United States and a stable industrial democracy are going to be easier than between the United States and an unstable developing country being run by a military junta. An unstable country will be more focused on establishing internal stability than on broader world concerns.

üîß Section 2: Foreign Policy Instruments

The tools and mechanisms of U.S. foreign policy

üìä Broadly vs. Sharply Focused Outputs

The decisions or outputs of U.S. foreign policy vary from presidential directives about conducting drone strikes to the size of the overall foreign relations budget passed by Congress. These outputs can be categorized into two types:

Two Types of Foreign Policy Outputs:

Broadly Focused Outputs:

  • Span multiple topics and organizations
  • Require large-scale spending
  • Take longer to implement
  • Are harder to reverse
  • Tend to have lasting impact

Sharply Focused Outputs:

  • Processed quickly
  • Often unilateral moves by the president
  • Have a shorter time horizon
  • Easier for subsequent decision-makers to reverse
  • Do not usually have lasting impact

üìú Broadly Focused Foreign Policy Outputs

üîç Click to explore: Public Laws

+25 points! Understanding legislation!

Public laws (sometimes called statutes) are policies that affect more than a single individual. All policies enacted by Congress and the president are public laws.

Many statutes affect what the government can do in the foreign policy realm, including:

  • The National Security Act: Governs how the government shares and stores information
  • The Patriot Act: Clarifies what the government may do in collecting information about people in the name of protecting the country
  • The Homeland Security Act of 2002: Authorized creation of the Department of Homeland Security
  • The War Powers Resolution: Passed in 1973 to reassert Congress in war-making
‚öñÔ∏è The War Powers Resolution

Passed in 1973 by a congressional override of President Richard Nixon's veto, the War Powers Resolution requires presidents to get approval from Congress to continue any military campaign beyond sixty days.

While intended to rein in executive power, the law effectively gave presidents two months to wage war however they wish before needing congressional authorization.

üîç Click to explore: International Agreements

+25 points! Understanding treaties and agreements!

International agreements represent another broad-based foreign policy instrument. The United States enters into international agreements with other countries for a variety of reasons and on a variety of subjects.

The U.S. Constitution outlines the treaty process in Article II: The president negotiates a treaty, the Senate consents by a two-thirds vote, and finally the president ratifies it.

However, today over 90 percent of international agreements are not treaties but rather executive agreements:

  • Sole executive agreements: Negotiated and approved by the president
  • Congressional-executive agreements: Negotiated by the president and approved by a simple majority of both houses (like NAFTA)
üîç Click to explore: Appointments

+25 points! Understanding appointments!

The last broad type of foreign policy output consists of the foreign policy appointments made when a new president takes office.

Most foreign policy‚Äìrelated appointments, such as secretary of state and various undersecretaries, as well as all ambassadors, must be confirmed by a majority vote of the Senate.

Presidents seek to nominate people who know the area to which they're being appointed and who will be loyal to the president rather than to the bureaucracy in which they might work.

üéØ Sharply Focused Foreign Policy Outputs

In addition to broadly focused outputs, many sharply focused decisions are primarily the province of the president. These demonstrate what Alexander Hamilton, writing in the Federalist Papers, called the "energy and dispatch" inherent in the presidency.

üîç Click to explore: Military Crises - The Cuban Missile Crisis

+25 points! Understanding crisis response!

Perhaps the most famous foreign policy emergency was the Cuban Missile Crisis in 1962. With the Soviet Union placing nuclear missiles in Cuba, just a few hundred miles from Florida, a Cold War standoff with the United States escalated.

The Soviets at first denied the existence of the missiles, but U.S. reconnaissance flights proved they were there. Some in the military pushed for aggressive action, while State Department officials favored a diplomatic route.

President John F. Kennedy took the recommendation of a special committee, implementing a naval blockade of Cuba that subtly forced the Soviets' hands. The Soviets agreed to remove their Cuban missiles, and the United States agreed six months later to remove its missiles from Turkey.

üîç Click to explore: Presidential Summits

+25 points! Understanding high-level diplomacy!

Presidential summits are another form of focused foreign policy output. Often held at Camp David, Maryland, these meetings bring together the president and other heads of state to address important issues.

Examples include:

  • Camp David Accords (1978): President Jimmy Carter, Egyptian President Anwar El Sadat, and Israeli Prime Minister Menachem Begin met privately for twelve days negotiating a peace process
  • Malta Summit (1989): President George H. W. Bush and Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev met, symbolizing the end of the Cold War
üîç Click to explore: Military Use of Force

+25 points! Understanding military action!

Since the 1941 Pearl Harbor attacks and the immediate declaration of war by Congress, all initial uses of military force have been authorized by the president. Congress has never acted to reverse presidential action in this area.

Military engagement can be:

  • Offensive: Such as the Iraq War in 2003 or the 1989 removal of Panamanian leader Manuel Noriega
  • Defensive: Responding to aggression, like the Persian Gulf War in 1991 (Operation Desert Storm)

A recent example was the U.S. role in enforcing a no-fly zone over Libya in 2011, which included kinetic strikes to protect anti-government forces.

üèõÔ∏è Section 3: Institutional Relations in Foreign Policy

How the branches of government share power in foreign affairs

‚öñÔ∏è Foreign Policy and Shared Power

Institutional relationships in foreign policy constitute a paradox. On the one hand, aspects of foreign policymaking engage multiple branches of government and a multiplicity of actors. On the other hand, foreign policymaking can sometimes call for nothing more than for the president to make a formal decision, quickly endorsed by the legislative branch.

The Constitutional Framework:

While presidents are more empowered by the Constitution in foreign than in domestic policy, they nonetheless must seek approval from Congress on a variety of matters. Chief among these is the basic budgetary authority needed to run foreign policy programs.

Even a sole executive agreement often requires subsequent funding from Congress in order to be carried out, and funding calls for majority support from the House and Senate.

Policy Output Presidential Role Congressional Role
Public Laws Proposes, signs into law Proposes, approves for passage
Treaties Negotiates, ratifies Senate consents (‚Öî vote)
Sole Executive Agreements Negotiates, approves None (unless funding required)
Declaration of War Proposes Approves by majority vote
Military Use of Force Carries out (60 days) Approves beyond 60 days
Appointments Nominates candidates Senate approves by majority

üìä The Two Presidencies Thesis

üîç Click to explore: The Two Presidencies Theory

+30 points! Understanding presidential power!

The Two Presidencies Thesis was developed by Professor Aaron Wildavsky. It suggests that there are two distinct presidencies‚Äîone for foreign policy and one for domestic policy‚Äîand that presidents are more successful in foreign than domestic policy.

Reasons for stronger foreign policy presidency:

  • The Constitution names the president as commander-in-chief, negotiator of treaties, and nominating authority for ambassadors
  • Presidents were gradually given authority to enter executive agreements without Senate consent
  • The War Powers Resolution effectively gave presidents two months to wage war
  • During the Cold War, Congress was willing to let the president lead as a single voice for the country
üîç Click to explore: Changes Since the Cold War

+30 points! Understanding modern changes!

The governing context has changed in two key ways since the Cold War:

1. End of the Cold War (1989-1990): The demolition of the Berlin Wall and disintegration of the Soviet Union removed the competitive superpower dynamic. This led to less of a "rally-behind-the-president" effect in foreign policy.

2. Political Polarization: Beginning in the 1980s and escalating in the 1990s, Democrats and Republicans became more polarized. The moderate members who could work together were edged out, making it more likely that the opposing party would challenge presidential initiatives.

Today, presidents are opposed more often in Congress, even on foreign policy votes they win. Still, because of their formal powers, presidents remain overall more successful on foreign policy than on domestic policy.

üë• The Many Actors in Foreign Policy

A variety of actors carry out the various and complex activities of U.S. foreign policy:

üîç Click to explore: White House Staff

+25 points! Understanding White House foreign policy!

White House staff members engaged in foreign policy have regular contact with the president:

  • National Security Advisor: Heads the National Security Council, the president's top foreign policy advisor
  • Director of National Intelligence: Oversees the entire intelligence community (created after 9/11)
  • Director of the CIA: Reports directly to the president
  • Chair of the Joint Chiefs of Staff: The president's top uniformed military officer
  • U.S. Trade Representative: Develops and directs international trade agenda
  • OMB Director: Develops the president's budget, including foreign policy funding
üîç Click to explore: Cabinet Officials

+25 points! Understanding cabinet roles!

Several important officials work in the broader executive branch:

  • Secretary of State: The nation's chief diplomat, serves in the president's cabinet, and oversees the Foreign Service
  • Secretary of Defense: Civilian (nonmilitary) head of the armed services at the Department of Defense
  • Secretary of Homeland Security: Oversees the massive Department of Homeland Security, critically important in foreign policy
üîç Click to explore: Congressional Leaders

+25 points! Understanding Congress's role!

In Congress, several leaders play key roles in foreign policy:

  • Speaker of the House and minority leader
  • Senate majority and minority leaders
  • Committee chairs and ranking members of key committees:

Key Committees:

  • House: Foreign Affairs Committee, Committee on Armed Services
  • Senate: Committee on Foreign Relations, Armed Services Committee

These committees hold regular hearings, consider budget authorizations, and debate the future of U.S. foreign policy.

üèõÔ∏è Why Congress Members Rarely Focus on Foreign Policy

While key leaders monitor foreign policy matters, individual representatives and senators do so much less often. Unless there is a foreign policy crisis, legislators tend to focus on domestic matters.

Why the Focus on Domestic Policy?

There is not much to be gained with constituents by pursuing foreign policy matters. Domestic policy matters resonate more strongly with voters at home. A sluggish economy, increasing health care costs, and crime matter more to them than U.S. policy toward North Korea.

In polls about the "most important problem" in the United States, fewer than 10 percent of respondents typically name a foreign policy topic.

However, some members do engage in foreign policy for several reasons:

  • Congressional party leaders speak on behalf of their party on all issues
  • Members with military bases in their districts have constituency reasons
  • Some have personal interest in foreign policy matters
  • Some have ambitions to move into executive branch foreign policy positions

üåê Section 4: Approaches to Foreign Policy

Classic and contemporary schools of thought

üìö Classic Approaches to Foreign Policy

A variety of traditional concepts of foreign policy remain helpful today as we consider the proper role of the United States in foreign affairs. These include isolationism, the idealism versus realism debate, liberal internationalism, hard versus soft power, and the grand strategy.

üîç Click to explore: Isolationism

+30 points! Understanding isolationism!

Isolationism is an approach whereby a country stays out of foreign entanglements and keeps to itself. From the end of the Revolutionary War until the early twentieth century, this was a popular stance in U.S. foreign policy.

Among the founders, Thomas Jefferson especially advocated for isolationism, believing the United States stood a better chance of becoming truly free by keeping to itself. In his farewell address, President George Washington famously warned against "entangling alliances."

The United States was pulled into world affairs with World War I, but isolationist sentiment returned between the wars. After World War II, the U.S. engaged as a superpower, though isolationist ideas never fully disappeared.

Modern isolationists like Senator Jesse Helms (R-NC) opposed the U.S. being the "policeman" of the world, while Ron Paul and Rand Paul ran isolationist presidential campaigns.

üîç Click to explore: Liberal Internationalism

+30 points! Understanding internationalism!

At the other end of the spectrum is liberal internationalism, which advocates a foreign policy approach in which the United States becomes proactively engaged in world affairs.

Its adherents assume that liberal democracies must take the lead in creating a peaceful world by cooperating as a community of nations and creating effective world structures such as the United Nations.

A classic example is President Woodrow Wilson, who sought a League of Nations to voluntarily save the world after World War I.

üîç Click to explore: Idealism vs. Realism

+30 points! Understanding the great debate!

Idealists assume the best in others and see it as possible for countries to run the world together, with open diplomacy, freedom of the seas, free trade, and no militaries. Everyone will take care of each other.

Realists assume that others will act in their own self-interest and hence cannot necessarily be trusted. They want a healthy military and contracts between countries in case others want to wiggle out of their commitments.

Liberal internationalism contains elements of both: idealism because the U.S. assumes other countries will put their best foot forward, and realism because the U.S. approaches foreign relationships with open eyes and emphasis on self-preservation.

üéØ Grand Strategy

Grand strategy involves employing all available diplomatic, economic, and military resources to advance the national interest. It relies on developing clear strategic directions for U.S. foreign policy and the methods to achieve those goals, often with military capability attached.

The U.S. foreign policy plan in Europe and Asia after World War II reflects this approach. The United States built military bases in Italy, Germany, Spain, England, Belgium, Japan, Guam, and Korea, often under multinational arrangements like NATO.

üîÑ More Recent Schools of Thought

Two events caused many to rethink traditional approaches: the debacle of U.S. involvement in Vietnam and the rise of new enemies like terrorist organizations.

üîç Click to explore: Neoconservatism

+30 points! Understanding neoconservatism!

Neoconservatives believe that rather than exercising restraint and always using international organizations, the United States should aggressively use its might to promote its values and ideals around the world.

The aggressive use (or threat) of hard power is the core value of neoconservatism. Acting unilaterally is acceptable, as is adopting a preemptive strategy in which the United States intervenes militarily before the enemy can make its move.

Examples include:

  • 1980s U.S. campaigns in Central America under President Reagan
  • The Iraq War of 2003 led by President George W. Bush and Vice President Dick Cheney
  • The use of drones as counterterrorism weapons during the Obama administration
üîç Click to explore: Neo-Isolationism & Selective Engagement

+30 points! Understanding modern approaches!

Neo-isolationism advocates keeping free of foreign entanglements, though no advanced industrial democracy completely separates itself from the rest of the world. In the twenty-first century, neo-isolationism means distancing the United States from international organizations that get in the way.

Selective engagement is the strategy of retaining a strong military presence and remaining engaged across the world through alliances and formal installations to protect national security interests, while seeking to avoid being the world's police officer.

A hybrid approach that uses multiple schools of thought as circumstances warrant may be the wave of the future. President Obama often took a hybrid approach‚Äîinternationalist when building coalitions, but neoconservative when sending drones to take out terrorist targets.

üåè U.S. Foreign Policy with Russia and China

üîç Click to explore: Relations with Russia

+25 points! Understanding U.S.-Russia relations!

The Soviet Union dissolved into many component parts after the Cold War, including Russia and various former Soviet republics. The U.S. approach has been to encourage the adoption of democracy and economic reforms in these former Eastern bloc countries.

Many now align with the EU and even NATO. Under President Vladimir Putin, Russia is again trying to strengthen its power on the country's western border, testing expansionism while invoking Russian nationalism.

U.S.-Russia relations have been frosty since Putin ascended, save for President Trump's efforts to befriend him. President Biden has taken a more emphatic stand with Russia on issues of wrongdoing and questions regarding Russian influence in U.S. elections.

üîç Click to explore: Relations with China

+25 points! Understanding U.S.-China relations!

China is the potential rival superpower of the future. A communist state that has encouraged economic development, China has been growing and modernizing for more than thirty years. Its nearly 1.4 billion citizens are stepping onto the world economic stage.

Chinese investors lend money to the U.S. government regularly, as U.S. domestic borrowing capacity is pushed to the limit.

The Tiananmen Square massacre in 1989, where thousands of pro-democracy protestors were arrested and many killed, raised concerns about human rights in China.

President Trump imposed tariffs on Chinese goods, straining the relationship. President Biden has continued tough stances on China. This souring relationship has affected U.S. universities where several Confucius Institutes have been shut down.

üìú Vietnam and the Change in Foreign Policy Thinking

Containment was the U.S. foreign policy goal of limiting the spread of communism during the Cold War. In Vietnam, the United States supported democratic South Vietnam, while the Soviet Union supported communist North Vietnam.

The U.S. military approach of battlefield engagement did not translate well to the jungles of Vietnam, where "guerilla warfare" predominated. U.S. forces withdrew from South Vietnam in 1973, and Saigon fell to the communists eighteen months later.

The Vietnam debacle caused many skeptics to become pessimistic about liberal internationalism, leading them to become neoconservatives on foreign policy.
