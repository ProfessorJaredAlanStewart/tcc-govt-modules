üì∞ Section 1: What Is the Media?

Freedom of the press and an independent media are important dimensions of a liberal society and a necessary part of a healthy democracy. "No government ought to be without censors," said Thomas Jefferson, "and where the press is free, no one ever will."

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Describe the main functions of the media in a free society
  • Compare different media formats and their respective audiences

üì∫ Media Basics

Ours is an exploding media system. What started as print journalism was subsequently supplemented by radio coverage, then network television, followed by cable television. Now, with the addition of the Internet, blogs and social media‚Äîa set of applications or web platforms that allow users to immediately communicate with one another‚Äîgive citizens a wide variety of sources for instant news of all kinds.

The Internet also allows citizens to initiate public discussion by uploading images and video for viewing, such as videos documenting interactions between citizens and the police. Provided we are connected digitally, we have a bewildering amount of choices for finding information about the world.

üì∞ Mass Media
The collection of all forms of media that communicate information to the general public, including television, print, radio, and Internet. One of the primary reasons citizens turn to the media is for news. We expect the media to cover important political and social events and information in a concise and neutral manner.
üîç Click to explore: Key Roles in Media Organizations

+30 points! üåü To accomplish its work, the media employs a number of people in varied positions:

  • Journalists and Reporters: Responsible for uncovering news stories by keeping an eye on areas of public interest, like politics, business, and sports. They research background information and interview people to create complete and balanced accounts.
  • Editors: Work in the background of the newsroom, assigning stories, approving articles or packages, and editing content for accuracy and clarity.
  • Publishers: People or companies that own and produce print or digital media. They oversee both the content and finances of the publication.
  • Producers: Oversee the production and finances of visual media, like television, radio, and film.
üé≠ Public Relations
Communication carried out to improve the image of companies, organizations, or candidates for office. Public relations is not a neutral information form. While journalists write stories to inform the public, a public relations spokesperson is paid to help an individual or organization get positive press. PR materials normally appear as press releases or paid advertisements. Some less reputable publications, however, publish paid articles under the news banner, blurring the line between journalism and public relations.

üì± Media Types and Demographics

Each form of media has its own complexities and is used by different demographics. Millennials (born 1981-1996) and Generation Z (born 1997-2012) are more likely to get news and information from social media, such as Instagram, TikTok, Twitter, and YouTube, while Baby Boomers (born 1946-1964) are most likely to get their news from television, either national broadcasts or local news.

üè¢ Media Conglomerates and Consolidation

Most media are controlled by a limited number of conglomerates. A conglomerate is a corporation made up of a number of companies, organizations, and media networks. In the 1980s, more than fifty companies owned the majority of television and radio stations and networks. By 2011, six conglomerates controlled most of the broadcast media in the United States.

üîç Click to explore: The Shrinking Media Landscape

+30 points! üìâ Media consolidation has accelerated dramatically:

  • 1983: 50 companies owned 90% of U.S. media
  • 2011: Six conglomerates controlled most broadcast media: CBS Corporation, Comcast, Time Warner, 21st Century Fox, Viacom, and The Walt Disney Company
  • 2019: Disney-Fox merger and CBS-Viacom merger reduced this to just 4 major conglomerates
  • 2021: Four conglomerates (Comcast, Disney, ViacomCBS, Warner Bros. Discovery) control 90% of U.S. media

The Walt Disney Company owns ABC Television Network, ESPN, A&E, Lifetime, Disney Channel, and Disney+. Viacom owns BET, Comedy Central, MTV, Nickelodeon, and VH1.

üèõÔ∏è Functions of the Media

The media exist to fill a number of functions. Whether the medium is a newspaper, radio, or television newscast, a corporation behind the scenes must bring in revenue and pay for the cost of the product. Revenue comes from advertising and sponsors. But corporations will not pay for advertising if there are no viewers or readers. So all programs and publications need to entertain, inform, or interest the public.

üîç Click to explore: The Fourth Estate

+30 points! üèõÔ∏è The media's critical functions in democracy:

  • Watchdog Role: The media are watchdogs of society and public officials. Some refer to the media as the "fourth estate," with the branches of government being the first three estates and the media equally participating as the fourth.
  • Accountability: This role helps maintain democracy and keeps the government accountable for its actions, even if a branch of government is reluctant to open itself to public scrutiny.
  • Information Gateway: The media keeps an eye on what is happening and sounds an alarm when the public needs to pay attention.
üìã Agenda Setting
The media's ability to choose which issues or topics deserve public discussion. For example, in the early 1980s, famine in Ethiopia drew worldwide attention after western media discovered it, which resulted in increased charitable giving. Yet the famine had been going on for a long time before media coverage brought it to public attention.

üìú Section 2: The Evolution of the Media

The evolution of the media has been fraught with concerns and problems. Accusations of mind control, bias, and poor quality have been thrown at the media on a regular basis. Yet the growth of communications technology allows people today to find more information more easily than any previous generation.

üì∞ Print Media: The Foundation

Early news was presented to local populations through the print press. While several colonies had printers and occasional newspapers, high literacy rates combined with the desire for self-government made Boston a perfect location for the creation of a newspaper, and the first continuous press was started there in 1704.

üîç Click to explore: Newspapers and the Revolutionary War

+30 points! üìú The role of newspapers in American independence:

  • Stamp Tax of 1765: Raised costs for publishers, causing several newspapers to fold
  • After Repeal (1766): Editors began questioning British rule over the colonies
  • Revolutionary Role: Newspapers took part in informing citizens of British misdeeds and inciting attempts to revolt
  • Growing Readership: Readership increased to nearly 40,000 homes (among a total population of two million)
  • Daily Papers: Sprang up in large cities to meet demand for news
üó≥Ô∏è Party Press Era
Period during the 1780s in which newspaper content was biased by political partisanship. The publication of The Federalist Papers, as well as the Anti-Federalist Papers, moved the nation into this era where party loyalty dominated editorial content. Subscriptions and advertisements did not fully cover printing costs, so political parties stepped in to support presses that aided their policies. Papers began printing party propaganda and messages, even publicly attacking political leaders like George Washington.
üîç Click to explore: The Penny Press and Yellow Journalism

+30 points! üì∞ Print media evolution 1830-1900:

  • 1830-1860: Machines made newspaper production faster and less expensive. Benjamin Day's New York Sun used the linotype machine for mass production.
  • Penny Press Papers: Contained more gossip than news but were affordable at one penny per issue
  • 1841: Horace Greeley's New-York Tribune aimed for high journalistic standards
  • Yellow Journalism: Joseph Pulitzer's New York World included sensational front-page news, cartoons, and pictures. This style became known as yellow journalism‚Äîsensationalized coverage of scandals and human interest stories.
  • 1896: Adolph Ochs purchased the New York Times with the goal of creating a dignified newspaper with important news rather than gossip
üîç Muckraking
News coverage focusing on exposing corrupt business and government practices. With the arrival of the Progressive Era, the media began this investigative journalism. Upton Sinclair's serialized novel The Jungle led to changes in how industrial workers were treated. The Pure Food and Drug Act and other laws were passed to protect consumers from unsafe food processing practices.

In the twenty-first century, newspapers have struggled to stay financially stable. Print media earned $46 billion from ads in 2012, but only $20.5 billion in 2020. Many newspapers now maintain both print and Internet presence. One-fifth of small-town newspapers have folded, and state legislative press corps have shrunk considerably.

üìª Radio: A New Voice for Politics

Radio news made its appearance in the 1920s. The National Broadcasting Company (NBC) and the Columbia Broadcasting System (CBS) began running sponsored news programs and radio dramas. Comedy programs like Amos 'n' Andy and Easy Aces became popular during the 1930s as listeners sought humor during the Depression.

üîç Click to explore: The Golden Age of Radio

+30 points! üìª Radio transformed American life:

  • By 1940: Almost 83% of households had a radio, and most tuned in regularly
  • Rural America: Radio brought communications to rural communities with programs like the National Farm and Home Hour
  • Grand Ole Opry: WSM in Nashville began broadcasting this live music show, still the longest live broadcast radio show in U.S. history
  • Political Use: Warren Harding was the first president to regularly give speeches over radio
  • Herbert Hoover: Used radio to announce government programs on aid and unemployment relief

üéôÔ∏è Milestone: FDR's Fireside Chats

Franklin D. Roosevelt became famous for harnessing the political power of radio. On entering office in March 1933, President Roosevelt needed to quiet public fears about the economy and prevent people from removing their money from banks.

Eight days after assuming the presidency, he delivered his first radio speech: "My friends: I want to talk for a few minutes with the people of the United States about banking..."

Roosevelt spoke directly to the people and addressed them as equals. One listener described the chats as soothing, with the president acting like a father, sitting in the room with the family, cutting through the political nonsense. These "fireside chats" became an important way for him to promote his New Deal agenda and allowed him to expand both the government and the presidency beyond their traditional roles.

üîç Click to explore: Radio During World War II

+30 points! üéñÔ∏è World War II transformed radio news:

  • Demand for Updates: The constantly evolving war made newspapers too slow; people wanted immediate news
  • Edward R. Murrow: CBS reporter began reporting live about Germany's actions from Europe, including live coverage during Germany's aerial bombing of London
  • Government Guidelines: The White House placed guidelines on reporting classified information, making a legal exception to First Amendment protection
  • Voluntary Censorship: Newscasters agreed to suppress information about the atomic bomb and military movements until after events occurred

The Radio Act of 1927 created the Federal Radio Commission (FRC) to set standards, frequencies, and license stations. The Communications Act of 1934 replaced it with the Federal Communications Commission (FCC), which still regulates interstate communications today.

In the 1980s, talk stations began to gain ground on both AM and FM frequencies. By the 1990s, talk shows had gone national, showcasing broadcasters like Rush Limbaugh. Today, podcasting has exploded with 106.7 million U.S. listeners, with 77.9 million listening at least once per week.

üì∫ Television: The Visual Revolution

Television combined the best attributes of radio and pictures and changed media forever. The first official broadcast in the United States was President Franklin Roosevelt's speech at the opening of the 1939 World's Fair in New York. By the 1950s, the price of television sets had dropped, more stations were being created, and advertisers were buying spots.

‚öñÔ∏è Section 3: Regulating the Media

The Constitution gives Congress responsibility for promoting the general welfare. While it is difficult to define what this broad dictate means, Congress has used it to protect citizens from media content it deems inappropriate. Although the media are independent participants in the U.S. political system, their liberties are not absolute and there are rules they must follow.

üìú Media and the First Amendment

The U.S. Constitution was written in secrecy. Journalists were neither invited to watch the drafting, nor did the framers talk to the press about their disagreements and decisions. Once finished, however, the Constitution was released to the public and almost all newspapers printed it, along with commentary about the new form of government it proposed.

"Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the government for a redress of grievances."
‚Äî First Amendment to the U.S. Constitution
üîç Click to explore: John Peter Zenger and Press Freedom

+30 points! ‚öñÔ∏è The foundation of American press freedom:

  • 1733: John Peter Zenger began the New York Weekly Journal with the goal of exposing corruption in the colonial government
  • 1735: Colonial Governor William Cosby had Zenger arrested and charged with seditious libel
  • The Defense: Zenger's lawyers argued that a statement is not libelous if it can be proved true
  • The Verdict: Zenger was found not guilty, affirming the importance of a free press in the colonies
  • The Legacy: This case established a crucial precedent for press freedom that would later be enshrined in the First Amendment

Slander and Libel

The media do not have the right to commit slander (speaking false information with intent to harm a person or entity) or libel (printing false information with intent to harm). These acts constitute defamation of character that can cause loss of reputation and income.

üîç Click to explore: Standards for Libel and Slander

+30 points! ‚öñÔ∏è When can media be sued for defamation?

  • False Facts: Libel and slander occur only when false information is presented as fact. Opinions are protected.
  • Public vs. Private Figures: Courts have different standards depending on whether the claimant is public or private
  • Public Figure Standard: Must show the publisher acted in "reckless disregard" of the truth or with malicious intent
  • Private Individual Standard: Must show the author was negligent in not verifying information
  • New York Times v. Sullivan (1964): A police commissioner in Alabama sued over inaccurate statements. The Supreme Court applied a stringent test of malice for public figures.

Example: In 2010, Star magazine published "Addiction Nightmare: Katie Drug Shocker." Katie Holmes filed a $50 million lawsuit, and Star's parent company eventually settled with an apology.

Classified Material and Prior Restraint

The media have only a limited right to publish material the government says is classified. If a newspaper obtains classified material, the government may request certain material be redacted.

üö´ Prior Restraint
A government action that stops someone from doing something before they are able to do it (e.g., forbidding someone to publish a book they plan to release). The Supreme Court has ruled that while the government can impose prior restraint on the media, that right is very limited.
üîç Click to explore: The Pentagon Papers Case

+30 points! üì∞ A landmark case for press freedom:

  • Supreme Court Ruling: While the government can impose prior restraint, that right is very limited
  • Result: The newspapers were given the right to publish much of the study
  • Exceptions: Revelation of troop movements and names of undercover operatives are approved reasons for prior restraint
  • Geraldo Rivera Example (2003): FOX reporter was removed from Iraq after drawing a map showing troop locations and discussing future movements on live television

üì° Media and FCC Regulations

The liberties enjoyed by newspapers are overseen by the U.S. court system, while television and radio broadcasters are monitored by both the courts and a government regulatory commission.

üîç Click to explore: History of Broadcast Regulation

+30 points! üìª The evolution of broadcast regulation:

  • Radio Act of 1927: First attempt by Congress to regulate broadcast materials. Created the Federal Radio Commission (FRC) to set standards, frequencies, and license stations.
  • Communications Act of 1934: Created the Federal Communications Commission (FCC)‚Äîoriginally seven members, now five‚Äîto oversee radio, television, and telephone communications
  • FCC Powers: Requires stations to apply for licenses, follow advertising limits, provide public forum for discussion, and serve local and minority communities
  • Indecency Regulations: Laws that limit indecent and obscene material on public airwaves
  • Telecommunications Act of 1996: Dropped limits on station ownership, allowing networks to purchase large numbers of stations
‚è∞ Equal-Time Rule
An FCC policy that all candidates running for office must be given the same radio and television airtime opportunities. If WBNS in Columbus, Ohio agrees to sell Senator Marco Rubio thirty seconds of airtime, the station must also sell all other candidates in that race thirty seconds at the same price. This rate cannot be more than the station charges favored commercial advertisers.
üîç Click to explore: Equal-Time Rule in Practice

+30 points! üì∫ How the equal-time rule works:

  • News Exception: The FCC waives the rule if coverage is purely news‚Äîinterviews during rallies and short documentaries are exempt
  • 2003 California Recall: Arnold Schwarzenegger and Gary Coleman were candidates. TV stations didn't run their shows to avoid equal-time requirements for all 135 candidates!
  • State of the Union: Broadcasting the president's speech can trigger equal-time provisions. Opposing parties now use their time immediately following for an official rebuttal.
‚öñÔ∏è Fairness Doctrine (defunct)
A 1949 FCC policy that required holders of broadcast licenses to cover controversial issues in a balanced manner by providing listeners with information about all perspectives. The fairness doctrine ended in the 1980s after courts ruled it limited debate of controversial topics and placed the government in the role of editor. The FCC repealed it in 1987.

  • Safe Harbor: Indecent programs can only be broadcast between 10 p.m. and 6 a.m.

‚òÄÔ∏è Media and Transparency

The press has had some assistance in performing its muckraking duty. Laws that mandate federal and many state government proceedings and meeting documents be made available to the public are called sunshine laws.

üìã Freedom of Information Act (FOIA)
A federal statute passed in 1966 and signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson that requires the executive branch of the U.S. government to provide information requested by citizens. It was intended to increase openness in the executive branch, which had been criticized for hiding information. Citizens wishing to obtain information may request documents from agencies, which may charge fees for collection and copying.
üîç Click to explore: FOIA in Practice

+30 points! üìÅ How FOIA works:

  • Exemptions: Human resource and medical records, national defense records, and material from confidential sources do not need to be disclosed
  • 1981: President Reagan exempted the CIA and FBI from FOIA requests
  • 2014: U.S. agencies received over 700,000 requests, many to the Departments of State and Defense

üìä Section 4: The Impact of the Media

In what ways can the media affect society and government? The media's primary duty is to present us with information and alert us when important events occur. This information may affect what we think and the actions we take. The media can also place pressure on government to act by signaling a need for intervention or showing that citizens want change.

üß† Media Effects and Bias Theories

Concerns about the effects of media on consumers and the existence and extent of media bias go back to the 1920s. Reporter and commentator Walter Lippmann noted that citizens have limited personal experience with government and the world and posited that the media, through their stories, place ideas in citizens' minds.

üîç Click to explore: Theories of Media Effects

+30 points! üìö How scholars have understood media influence:

  • Hypodermic Theory (1920s): The idea that information is "shot" into the receiver's mind and readily accepted. Based on Lippmann's observation that media ideas become part of citizens' frame of reference.
  • Minimal Effects Theory (1930s-40s): Studies found information was transmitted in two steps‚Äîone person reads news, then shares with friends. People listened to friends, not those they disagreed with. This led to the theory that media have little effect on citizens.
  • Cultivation Theory (1970s): Media develop a person's view of the world by presenting a perceived reality. What we see on a regular basis becomes our reality. Media can set norms by choosing what is covered or discussed.

The consensus: media have some effect, even if subtle.

üñºÔ∏è Framing
The process of giving a news story a specific context or background. The news often uses frames to place a story in context so the reader understands its importance or relevance. Yet, at the same time, framing affects the way the reader or viewer processes the story.
üîç Click to explore: Episodic vs. Thematic Framing

+30 points! üé¨ Two approaches to framing stories:

  • Episodic Framing: Focuses on isolated details or specifics rather than looking broadly at an issue. Example: Reporting current homeless statistics, interviewing a few homeless people, and looking at a city's current shelter investment.
  • Thematic Framing: Takes a broad look at an issue, examining how it has changed over time and what has led to it. Example: Examining homelessness as a nationwide problem, exploring reasons people become homeless, and discussing trends in cities' attempts to solve the problem.
  • Effect: Episodic frames may create more sympathy, while thematic frames may leave readers emotionally disconnected and less sympathetic
üéØ Priming
The process of predisposing readers or viewers to think a particular way. When media coverage focuses on certain issues, it predisposes the viewer to a particular perspective. If a newspaper article focuses on unemployment, struggling industries, and jobs moving overseas, the reader will have a negative opinion about the economy. If then asked about the president's job performance, the reader is primed to say no.

üó≥Ô∏è Coverage Effects on Governance and Campaigns

When it is spotty, the media's coverage of campaigns and government can sometimes affect the way government operates and the success of candidates. In 1972, the McGovern-Fraser reforms created a voter-controlled primary system, so party leaders no longer pick the presidential candidates. Now the media are seen as kingmakers and play a strong role in influencing who will become the nominees.

üîç Click to explore: Campaign Control and the "Bubble"

+30 points! üé™ How campaigns try to control media coverage:

  • 1984 Issue-of-the-Day: Reagan and Bush campaigns provided quotes and material on only one topic each day, limiting what journalists could cover
  • 1992: Both Bush and Clinton campaigns maintained carefully drawn candidate images by limiting photographers to photo opportunities at rallies
  • The "Bubble": The constant control of the media; journalists were less effective when in the campaign's bubble
  • Journalists Pushed Back: Reporters complained this was campaign advertising rather than journalism
  • Result: Journalists began analyzing campaigns without input from candidates
üêé Horse-Race Coverage
When modern journalists analyze campaigns and blunders or the overall race, rather than interviewing candidates or discussing their issue positions. Campaign coverage now focuses on the spectacle of the season‚Äîcolorful personalities, strange comments, lapses of memory, and embarrassing revelations are more likely to get air time than candidates' issue positions.
üîç Click to explore: The Shrinking Sound Bite

+30 points! üì∫ How candidate coverage has changed:

  • 1968: Average sound bite from Richard Nixon was 42.3 seconds
  • 2004: Sound bites had decreased to only 8 seconds
  • Content of Clips: 40% were attacks on opponents; only 30% contained information about candidate's issues or events
  • Visual Coverage: News showed images of candidates for an average of only 25 seconds while the newscaster discussed stories
  • 2012 Pew Study: 64% of stories focused on campaign strategy; only 9% covered domestic issues; 6% covered public records; 1% covered foreign policy
üê∫ Pack Journalism
When journalists follow one another rather than digging for their own stories. Television news discusses strategies and blunders with colorful examples. Newspapers focus on polls. This shallow press coverage has become common today.

üåç Coverage Effects on Society

The media choose what they want to discuss. This agenda setting creates a reality for voters and politicians that affects the way people think, act, and vote. Even if the crime rate is going down, citizens accustomed to reading stories about assault and other offenses still perceive crime to be an issue.

üîç Click to explore: Racial Framing in Media

+30 points! üìä Research on media's portrayal of race:

  • Crime Coverage: Local news shows were more likely to show pictures of criminals when they were African American, overrepresenting Black people as perpetrators and White people as victims
  • Latino Coverage: Latino people were underrepresented as both victims of crime and as police officers, while White people were overrepresented as both
  • Poverty Coverage: Network news misrepresents victims of poverty by using more images of African Americans than White people, leaving viewers believing African Americans are the majority of poor and unemployed
  • Magazine Study: Time and Newsweek showed images of young African Americans when discussing poverty, excluding the elderly, young, and White and Latino people‚Äîwho represent the true picture of poverty

Racial framing, even if unintentional, affects perceptions and policies. The perception that most recipients of welfare are working-age African Americans may have led some citizens to vote for candidates who promised to reduce welfare benefits. When survey respondents were shown a story of a White unemployed individual, 71% listed unemployment as one of the top three problems facing the United States, while only 53% did so if the story was about an unemployed African American.
